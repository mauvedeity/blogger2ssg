Another hurdle for Cryptocurrencies

As has been remarked elsewhere, I'm a proud nocoiner normie. But that doesn't mean that I'm not interested in the worst excesses of cryptocurrencies and friends.

But rather than simply go over the arguments that have been explained way better by others, I've been pondering a small piece of the situation. It's fascinating in a way, because crypto seems to be one of those ideas that fractally wrong - if you look at the big picture, it's a bad idea, and if you zoom down into tiny details, there are lots of bad ideas in there too.

One thing that occurred to me was that companies seem to be heading down the crypto currency route. Facebook/Meta has Diem, and other companies have theirs.

Now, I know that the company I work for doesn't have any stake in cryptocurrencies at the moment, which is all good. But what if it did? My contract of employment specifies how I'll be paid, which is nice. But say the following things happened:

  1. The company creates its own cryptocurrency, called for the sake of argument "WorkCoin";
  2. They decide that WorkCoin is now the currency used to denominate my salary;
  3. They decide to pay me only in WorkCoin, rather than by BACS into my UK bank account.

This presents me with a problem. Yes, technically I'm being paid in a currency. However, WorkCoin is only convertible to goods and services at the whim of my employer, amongst many other enormous disadvantages of crypto. This means that I could, for instance, be forced to buy goods and services only through my employer or merchants that they have an agreement with. If I wanted to shop at my local artisanal laptop wright, but can only pay in non-convertible WorkCoin, I wouldn't be able to.

This is a problem. But what can we do to prevent this nightmare scenario? To start answering the question, let us look back in time.

This scenario is in fact very similar to a situation that existed for many people in the UK many years ago. In a factory town, often a currency controlled by the factory became a de facto currency in the area. Let's call this "WorkCoin". Now, you can only buy food in the factory shop, as that's a business that accepts WorkCoin. You can't buy anything the factory doesn't sell, and if you're blacklisted by the factory, since all you hold is suddenly worthless WorkCoin, you can't do anything except slowly starve in the dark.

Fortunately, laws were passed in the 19th Century to prevent this practice, beginning with the Truck Act of 1831. The original Act was modified a few times, most notably in 1896 to broaden the scope of workers covered, 1906 to cover deductions from wages, and 1974 to permit payment by cheque. Essentially the basis of the law is that every employee can demand to be paid in "Coin of the Realm", and not by other means.

It seems to me interesting that crypto bros are basically re-inventing the history of finance, one catastrophe at a time, so I'm watching with interest to see when they get to this one. Pass the popcorn!
